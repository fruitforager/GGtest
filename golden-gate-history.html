Our Roots: The History of the Golden Gate Chapter

From a hand-cranked mimeograph machine to a thriving regional network of urban food forests mapping the microclimates of the Bay Area.

Introduction

Welcome to the Golden Gate Chapter of the California Rare Fruit Growers (CRFG). Today, we are proud to be part of the largest amateur fruit-growing organization in the world, with thousands of members across 35 countries.

While our members now cultivate thriving urban orchards throughout the central and maritime Bay Area, our story actually began more than half a century ago as a simple, passionate conversation between two extraordinary fruit visionaries separated by hundreds of miles.

The Spark:
A Soldier and an Engineer (1966–1968)

The story of the CRFG was born in 1966 out of the shared obsession of two men: Paul Thomson and John Riley.

The Southern Grower

Paul Thomson

A rugged ex-Marine, self-taught botanist, and organic farmer living in San Diego's North County. On his five-acre farm in Bonsall (and later his property "Edgehill" in Vista), Thomson was doing what many agricultural experts at the time claimed was impossible: successfully cultivating tropical and subtropical treats like cherimoyas, mangoes, lychees, and longans.

The Northern Innovator

John Riley

A brilliant Lockheed aerospace engineer based right here in Northern California (Santa Clara). He possessed an identical passion for rare, untried plants and brought a meticulous, scientific engineering mind to the art of plant propagation.

"They arrived without prejudice to what could not be grown... starting an agricultural paradigm shift right from their backyards."

In 1966, Riley visited Thomson in North San Diego County. Seeing the incredible diversity of subtropical fruits thriving there, both men recognized a massive gap in the horticultural world. Commercial agriculture ignored these fruits, and backyard gardeners interested in unusual edible plants had no reliable sources of information or community support.

Inspired in part by the Florida Rare Fruit Council, the duo began collaborating. Using a hand-cranked mimeograph machine, they compiled their shared insights, successes, and failures into a typed newsletter, mailing it out to a small network of about 20 to 30 fellow enthusiasts.

1968: The Birth of an Organization

As interest exploded, the duo realized that fruit hobbyists desperately needed a centralized clearinghouse to exchange knowledge, seeds, and wood. In November 1968, they officially founded the California Rare Fruit Growers, Inc.

The timing was perfect. The late 1960s brought a massive wave of newcomers moving to California. Crucially, these newcomers arrived "without prejudice to what could not be grown." Unbound by traditional agricultural dogmas, these backyard innovators started planting everything from passion fruit to pitahayas.

By 1971, the group had grown to nearly 400 members; by the mid-1970s, it surpassed 500. What started as a mimeographed newsletter evolved into our flagship bimonthly magazine, The Fruit Gardener, and our annual state conference known as the Festival of Fruit.

Spreading North:
The "Bay Area Chapter" (1983)

For its first 15 years, the CRFG's growth and early chapters remained almost exclusively south of the Tehachapi Mountains, which many considered the "Mecca" of exotic fruits. However, John Riley's Northern California roots ensured the Bay Area wouldn't be left behind.

The catalyst for our local history began in 1983, when Riley and Thomson decided it was time to establish a formal presence in Northern California. They helped organize the very first northern chapter in San Jose, aptly named the Bay Area Chapter, with Brent Thompson serving as its first chairman.

This single, monolithic chapter originally acted as the central hub for all fruit enthusiasts across the broader region. It drew an incredibly active, passionate group of growers from every corner of the Bay. One of their most significant early projects in the early 1980s was propagating and planting over 100 specimens of rare and prized fruit trees at Emma Prusch Farm Park in San Jose. This International Rare Fruit Orchard creates a living demonstration orchard that stands as a testament to those early years.

The Birth of the Golden Gate Chapter (1987)

"Our historic first meeting took place on September 12, 1987, at the South Berkeley Public Library."

As regional membership swelled throughout the mid-to-late 1980s, it became clear that a single chapter could no longer adequately serve the entire region. The vast geographical spread and distinct microclimates of the Bay Area required a more localized approach. Growing fruit in the hot inland valleys of the East Bay or the foggy coastal pockets of San Francisco required entirely different strategies than growing in the South Bay.

To better serve local neighborhoods, geographic clusters began to form localized hubs. This regional focus culminated in the official establishment of the Golden Gate Chapter, spearheaded by local visionary Katherine Pyle. Pyle recognized that the central and maritime Bay Area needed its own dedicated space to experiment.

Under her guidance, alongside her friend John Archer, the chapter held its historic first meeting on September 12, 1987, at the South Berkeley Public Library. That initial gathering brought together a tight-knit cluster of backyard innovators, establishing a focused mission to explicitly master the unique microclimates of Alameda, Contra Costa, Marin, San Francisco, San Mateo, and Solano counties.

A Legacy of Backyard Knowledge & Agroecology

Since that foundational first meeting, the Golden Gate Chapter has transformed the concept of rare fruit growing for local residents. Instead of focusing strictly on the classic southern heat-lovers, our chapter became a vital testing ground for identifying, trialing, and preserving varieties that thrive in cool, overcast summers or unique urban microclimates.

Chapter Chair John Valenzuela notes that rare fruit growing is a revelatory topic. It connects home gardeners directly to ethnobotany, agroecology, and the history of global trade. Many fruits we now take for granted—like Clementine mandarins or heirloom tomatoes—were once considered "rare" and unusual until small-scale, experimental growers proved their regional viability.

Our members often look at fruit growing through the lens of permaculture and "food forests"—designing highly efficient, multi-layered backyard ecosystems. This allows urban growers to pack maximum biodiversity into small yards by layering:

Ground Layer: Ground Covers & Trailing Vines — Strawberries, pumpkins, and trailing vines.
Shrub Layer: Shrubs and bushes — Peppers, blueberries, and huckleberries.
Canopy Layer: The understory and canopy — Exotic tree fruits like pineapple guavas (feijoas), sapotes, jujubes, kumquats, and cold-hardy Mexican avocados (like 'Mexicola' or 'Gwen') that defy old agricultural dogmas.

Over the decades, we have transitioned from a small group of local hobbyists swapping backyard secrets into a robust community. We act as a literal and digital "backyard fence"—a collaborative network where decades of specialized pomological knowledge is preserved and passed down to the next generation of urban orchardists.

What Defines a "Rare" Fruit?

At the Golden Gate Chapter, we honor the original mission statement penned by our founders. We define a fruit as "rare" if it meets any of these criteria:

  • It is not normally grown in our specific area or climatic zone.
  • It is scarce, heirloom, or endangered.
  • It exhibits unusual properties (like the taste-altering miracle fruit).
  • It is a newly introduced or discovered species.
  • It is an exceptional variety that is simply not grown commercially.

Carrying the Torch Today

From foggy coastal hillsides to sizzling inland valleys, the Golden Gate Chapter region represents an incredible playground for fruit-growing experimentation. While our founders began by testing the limits of tropicals in Southern California, we continue that spirit of adventure in the North—constantly pushing the boundaries of what can be grown in our own backyards.

Through education, preservation, and the sharing of plant material, the Golden Gate Chapter keeps Paul Thomson and John Riley's original 1968 vision alive. We invite you to join our regular meetings, featuring expert speakers, orchard tours, fruit tastings, and workshops that highlight the richness of our community.

You can also take part in our long-standing annual traditions:

  • Our popular Annual Golden Gate Scion Exchange (where beginners and experts alike choose from hundreds of varieties of dormant fruit tree cuttings).
  • Exclusive field trips guided orchard tours classes and ands-on workshops.
  • Seasonal fruit tastings from member orchards.
