Our Roots: The History of the Golden Gate Chapter

From a hand-cranked mimeograph machine to a thriving regional network of urban food forests mapping the microclimates of the Bay Area.

Introduction

Welcome to the Golden Gate Chapter of the California Rare Fruit Growers (CRFG)! Today, we are proud to be part of the largest amateur fruit-growing organization in the world, with thousands of members across 35 countries.

While our members now cultivate thriving urban orchards throughout the central and maritime Bay Area, our story actually began more than half a century ago as a simple, passionate conversation between two extraordinary fruit visionaries separated by hundreds of miles.

The Spark: A Soldier and an Engineer (1966–1968)

The story of the CRFG was born in 1966 out of the shared obsession of two men: Paul Thomson and John Riley.

The Southern Grower

Paul Thomson

A rugged ex-Marine, self-taught botanist, and organic farmer living in San Diego’s North County. On his five-acre farm in Bonsall (and later his property “Edgehill” in Vista), Thomson was doing what many agricultural experts at the time claimed was impossible: successfully cultivating tropical and subtropical treats like cherimoyas, mangoes, lychees, and longans.

The Northern Innovator

John Riley

A brilliant Lockheed aerospace engineer based right here in Northern California (Santa Clara). He possessed an identical passion for rare, untried plants and brought a meticulous, scientific engineering mind to the art of plant propagation.

"They arrived without prejudice to what could not be grown... starting an agricultural paradigm shift right from their backyards."

In 1966, Riley visited Thomson in North San Diego County. Seeing the incredible diversity of subtropical fruits thriving there, both men recognized a massive gap in the horticultural world. Commercial agriculture ignored these fruits, and backyard gardeners interested in unusual edible plants had no reliable sources of information or community support.

Inspired in part by the Florida Rare Fruit Council, the duo began collaborating. Using a hand-cranked mimeograph machine, they compiled their shared insights, successes, and failures into a typed newsletter, mailing it out to a small network of about 20 to 30 fellow enthusiasts.

1968: The Birth of an Organization

As interest exploded, the duo realized that fruit hobbyists desperately needed a centralized clearinghouse to exchange knowledge, seeds, and wood. In November 1968, they officially founded the California Rare Fruit Growers, Inc.
